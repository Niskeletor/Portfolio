In Frank Herbert's *Dune*, the Landsraad is the council of noble houses — each with its own territory, resources and responsibilities, all bound by a common law. No single house controls everything. Power is distributed, federated, governed by protocol.

That's the mental model behind my personal platform.

I didn't set out to build a themed homelab. I set out to solve real problems — reliability, automation, hardware independence — and the naming system emerged naturally as the infrastructure grew. The Dune universe just happened to be the perfect metaphor for what I was building: a federation of nodes, each with a defined role, all operating under a shared set of rules.

This is the story of how Landsraad works.

---

## The Houses of the Landsraad

Every node in the cluster has a name from astronomy or the Dune universe. But the naming isn't arbitrary — each name reflects the node's role and character.

### House Atreides — The Foundation (`atreides`, Raspberry Pi 4)

House Atreides in *Dune* is the noble house known for its honor, loyalty and strategic importance. In Landsraad, **Atreides** is the Raspberry Pi 4 — the most critical node in the entire infrastructure.

It runs the services that everything else depends on:
- **Pi-hole** — DNS and DHCP for the entire network. If Atreides goes down, nothing resolves.
- **Vaultwarden** — the password vault. Every credential in the empire lives here.
- **WireGuard** — the VPN gateway. Remote access to the entire platform.
- **Home Assistant** — home automation layer.
- **Cloudflare DDNS** — the platform's connection to the outside world.

A Raspberry Pi 4 running critical infrastructure sounds fragile. It isn't — it's the most reliable machine in the cluster. It consumes ~7W, has 7.6GB RAM (84% available), and has been running continuously for months. The constraint of low power forces simplicity, and simplicity is reliability.

### The Constellation Cluster — The Computing Nodes

The three Proxmox nodes are named after galaxies and constellations. Fitting: they are the physical universe where everything runs.

**Andromeda** is the entry point — an Intel i5-7500 with 15GB RAM. It hosts the K3s master node and the primary VMs. RAM is at 80% utilization, which means it's working hard but still headroom exists.

**Triangulum** is the storage specialist — an AMD Ryzen 5 5560U with 12GB RAM and ~17TB of raw storage. It hosts DUNE, the media VM. The Triangulum Galaxy is small but dense — same profile as this node.

**Orion** is the titan — an Intel Xeon E5-2630L with 62GB RAM and ~48TB storage spread across SSDs and HDDs. It runs TrueNAS (the storage backbone of the empire) and has significant headroom for expansion. Orion the hunter: powerful, patient, waiting.

**Perseus** is the newest addition — an Intel Xeon E5-2678v3 with 251GB RAM. Its purpose is singular and specific: it runs **Omnius**, the AI node. More on that later.

### The Worlds (VMs)

Each VM is a planet — a contained world with its own purpose and environment.

**DUNE** is the media planet. Named after the desert world of Arrakis, it runs the entire media stack: Jellyfin, Immich, Nextcloud, Nginx Proxy Manager, Tailscale. Everything flows through DUNE. The spice — in this case, media — must flow.

**Betelgeuse** is TrueNAS Scale — the storage star. Named after one of the largest known stars, it provides ~60TB of storage to the entire platform via NFS and SMB shares. All persistent data in the K3s cluster lands here.

**Omnius** is the AI thinking machine. In *Legends of Dune* (the Brian Herbert prequels), Omnius is the network of interconnected machine intelligences. In Landsraad, it's a VM with GPU passthrough running a full AI inference stack — more on this in its own section.

**Harkonnen** is the automation workhorse. House Harkonnen in *Dune* is ruthless and industrial — always extracting maximum output. This Debian server runs 29 Docker containers: the *arr stack (Sonarr, Radarr, Prowlarr), N8N, Wiki.js, Vikunja, Linkwarden. If Atreides is the foundation and DUNE is the experience layer, Harkonnen is the engine room.

## The Platform Architecture

Three architectural principles govern how Landsraad operates:

### 1. Separation of concerns by node

Each node has a defined specialty. This isn't just naming — it's operational design:

```
Atreides  → Critical network services (DNS, VPN, secrets)
Andromeda → Compute and Kubernetes control plane
Triangulum → Media storage and streaming
Orion     → Mass storage (TrueNAS) and compute reserve
Perseus   → AI inference (GPU workloads, isolated)
Harkonnen → Automation and productivity services
DUNE      → User-facing media services and reverse proxy
```

When a service misbehaves, the blast radius is contained. When a node needs maintenance, the other nodes continue operating. This is the distributed systems principle applied at home.

### 2. GitOps as the law of the land

The K3s cluster is managed entirely through ArgoCD. No manual `kubectl apply`. No SSH to patch a running service. The Git repository is the source of truth — every service definition, every resource configuration, every secret reference lives there.

The operational model:
1. Write or modify a manifest in the GitOps repo
2. Push to main
3. ArgoCD detects the change and reconciles the cluster state
4. Service is deployed, updated or removed — automatically

This means the cluster state is always auditable, always reversible, and always documented. `git log` is the deployment history. `git revert` is the rollback mechanism.

### 3. Observability as a first-class concern

VictoriaMetrics collects metrics from every node and service. Grafana provides dashboards for resource saturation, service health and storage capacity. Zabbix handles host-level monitoring and alerting.

The goal isn't to have dashboards — it's to have **actionable signals**. An alert that fires without a clear action is noise. Every alert in Landsraad has a corresponding runbook: what it means, what to check, what to do.

## Omnius — The AI Layer

The newest and most experimental node in the empire.

Perseus runs a single VM: **Omnius**. The hardware is a Xeon E5-2678v3 with 251GB RAM and an **RTX 5060 Ti with 16GB VRAM** passed through via VFIO. This is local AI inference — no API calls, no cloud bills, no data leaving the network.

The stack:

**Ollama** serves 16 LLM models locally — from general-purpose models like Qwen3 14B and Gemma3 27B to reasoning models like Phi4 and DeepSeek-R1. Two custom models (`parody-phi4` and `parody-deepseek`) are fine-tuned for a separate project.

**Open WebUI** provides a ChatGPT-like interface for interacting with any of the local models.

**ComfyUI** handles image generation — FLUX.1-dev FP8 with a custom LoRA for artistic styles.

**Kokoro TTS** provides text-to-speech with 30+ multilingual voices via API.

The choice to run AI inference locally was about control and privacy, not cost optimization. Every conversation, every generated image, every synthesized voice stays within the network perimeter.

## What This Platform Enables

The honest answer to "why build this?" isn't "because I can." It's because operating a platform at this scale — even at home, even without SLAs — teaches things that labs and courses don't:

**You learn to operate, not just deploy.** When Betelgeuse fills up, you're not doing a tutorial exercise — you're doing capacity planning under pressure. When Pi-hole has a DNS issue at 11pm, you're not reading about incident response — you're doing it.

**You learn that complexity has a cost.** 50+ services across multiple nodes means 50+ services that can fail, need updates, have dependencies. The answer isn't fewer services — it's better automation, better observability, better runbooks.

**You learn that platform thinking is different from sysadmin thinking.** A sysadmin fixes what's broken. A platform engineer asks: why did this break, how do we prevent it, and how do we make the next person's life easier when it breaks again?

Landsraad isn't a collection of services I deployed and forgot. It's a living system I operate, monitor, and improve — under real constraints, with real consequences when things go wrong.

## What's Next

The empire, as always, is expanding:

- **Perseus integration**: migrating Omnius from its temporary isolated network to the main cluster network and joining Perseus to the Proxmox cluster
- **K3s expansion**: activating the stopped worker nodes to give the cluster more capacity
- **SLO implementation**: defining formal SLIs and error budgets for critical services — treating the platform like a product, not a side project
- **Harkonnen relief**: the disk is at 91% capacity. This is a known issue. The fix is migrating heavy services to Orion's abundant storage. The delay is intentional: I want to do it right with proper volume migration, not just `rsync` and hope.

The spice must flow. And in Landsraad, the spice is uptime.
